The Challenge

What I Was Given

  • Three isolated screens from middle of flow
  • Generic copy to rewrite
  • Focus on "clarity and tone"

What I Delivered

  • Complete user workflow system
  • Role-based content architecture
  • Working prototype + strategy docs

Why go this far? Content effectiveness depends on flow design. You can't optimize individual screens without understanding the complete user journey and business context.

Strategic Problems I Identified

1

Missing Flow Context

The three screens were from the middle of a workflow. Copy on Screen 2 must build from what users experienced on Screen 1. Without this context, even perfect copy fails to guide users effectively.

2

User State Misalignment

An approver reviewing payments has a completely different mental state than an analyst setting up schedules. Same content can't serve both user types effectively.

3

Systems Gap

The brief asked about consistency across dashboards and notifications, but without seeing the full experience, I couldn't design scalable content patterns.

User-Centered Approach

Understanding User States

Enterprise users are busy and stressed. They're handling multiple priorities before they even reach our interface.

Key insight: User state matters as much as user type. The same person acts differently when initiating vs. approving payments.

Scalable Content Patterns

Designed for Reusability

  • Approval Language: Adapts based on amount thresholds and company policies
  • Recurrence Terminology: Consistent across setup, dashboards, and notifications
  • Error Prevention: Reusable patterns for high-stakes workflows
  • Audit Trail Language: Satisfies compliance without overwhelming users

Result: Content patterns that scale across the platform, reducing design debt and improving user experience consistency.
